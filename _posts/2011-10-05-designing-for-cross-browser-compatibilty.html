--- layout: post title: Designing for Cross-Browser Compatibilty categories: [] tags: [] status: draft type: post published: false meta: _edit_last: '1' --- I think every engineer should be prepared to answer the question: how does it look in IE7? No one enjoys supporting ancient, quirky browsers, but that's the reality we program in. Even if you work at the lucky minority of companies that don't have to support non-modern browsers, you should at least know what does and doesn't work in other browsers. From my experience, answering the cross-browser question is a lot easier when you do it from the very beginning. Most of the time, you can build a browser-proof website/application in your first attempt without doing a whole lot of additional browser checking. The trick is to constantly be aware of the things that do and don't work across browsers. Here are some tips I like to keep in the back of my mind:

For Browser-Proof HTML/CSS

1. Get a Good Reset Stylesheet

Enough can't be said about getting a solid reset stylesheet. When you're programming across different browsers, a reset stylesheet really helps to level the playing field. The rest of the tips for this section assume that you're using a reset stylesheet, since it usually alleviates a majority of the pain that comes from coding cross-browser compatible HTML/CSS.

For a more complete breakdown of how clears work, I recommend checking out the clearing article on quirksmode.org.

Browser-Proof JavaScript

1. Watch out for trailing commas

This is a pretty common one, but it's worth reiterating. IE7 throws a massive error when you have trailing commas in arrays. For instance, this will break in IE7: 
var array = [
    one,
    two,
    three,
]
The errors won't always be clear, but that that last comma is what's destroying your code. Always run jsLint where you can, and jUnit if you're privy to javascript unit testing.
